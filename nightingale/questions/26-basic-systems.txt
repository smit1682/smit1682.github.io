Q: Which type of muscle tissue is striated in appearance yet functions under involuntary control?
A) Smooth muscle
B) Skeletal muscle
C) Both skeletal and smooth
D) Cardiac muscle
Ans: D
Topic: Basic Sciences
Diff: easy
Exp: Cardiac muscle shows cross-striations like skeletal muscle but, like smooth muscle, is controlled involuntarily by the autonomic nervous system and intrinsic pacemakers. Skeletal muscle is striated and voluntary, while smooth muscle is non-striated and involuntary.

Q: The functional contractile unit of skeletal muscle, lying between two adjacent Z-lines, is the
A) Sarcomere
B) Myofibril
C) Sarcoplasmic reticulum
D) Sarcolemma
Ans: A
Topic: Basic Sciences
Diff: medium
Exp: The sarcomere is the region between two Z-lines and is the basic repeating contractile unit of striated muscle. According to the sliding filament theory, actin and myosin filaments slide past one another to shorten each sarcomere during contraction.

Q: The carpometacarpal joint at the base of the thumb is classified as which type of synovial joint?
A) Pivot joint
B) Saddle joint
C) Hinge joint
D) Ball-and-socket joint
Ans: B
Topic: Basic Sciences
Diff: medium
Exp: The thumb carpometacarpal joint is the classic example of a saddle joint, whose reciprocally concave and convex surfaces allow flexion, extension, abduction, adduction and opposition. This wide range of movement gives humans their characteristic thumb dexterity.

Q: Which cranial nerve carries impulses for the special sense of vision?
A) Oculomotor nerve, III
B) Abducens nerve, VI
C) Optic nerve, II
D) Trochlear nerve, IV
Ans: C
Topic: Basic Sciences
Diff: easy
Exp: The optic nerve, cranial nerve II, is purely sensory and transmits visual impulses from the retina to the brain. The oculomotor, trochlear and abducens nerves are motor nerves that move the eyeball rather than mediate vision itself.

Q: The principal nerve carrying sensation from the face and supplying the muscles of mastication is the
A) Glossopharyngeal nerve, IX

Ans: C
Topic: Basic Sciences

Exp: The trigeminal nerve is the chief sensory nerve of the face and also provides motor supply to the muscles of mastication. The facial nerve, in contrast, supplies the muscles of facial expression rather than chewing.

Q: Which of the following is an effect of parasympathetic, or vagal, stimulation?
A) Dilation of the pupil
B) Bronchodilation
C) Increased gastrointestinal motility and secretion
D) Acceleration of heart rate
Ans: C
Topic: Basic Sciences
Diff: medium
Exp: Parasympathetic activity promotes rest-and-digest functions, increasing gut motility, glandular secretion and salivation. It also constricts the pupil, slows the heart and causes bronchoconstriction, which are the opposite of sympathetic actions.

Q: The outermost and toughest of the three meningeal layers covering the brain and spinal cord is the
A) Arachnoid mater

C) Dura mater
D) Pia mater
Ans: C
Topic: Basic Sciences
Diff: easy
Exp: The three meninges from outside inward are the dura mater, arachnoid mater and pia mater. The dura is the thick, fibrous outermost layer, while the delicate pia mater adheres closely to the surface of the brain and cord.

Q: Coordination of voluntary movements and maintenance of posture and balance is chiefly the function of the
A) Medulla oblongata
B) Cerebellum
C) Hypothalamus
D) Thalamus
Ans: B
Topic: Basic Sciences
Diff: easy
Exp: The cerebellum coordinates the timing and smoothness of voluntary movement and helps maintain equilibrium and posture. Damage to it produces ataxia, incoordination and disturbances of balance rather than paralysis.

Q: Which sequence correctly represents the components of a typical reflex arc?
A) Receptor, afferent neuron, centre, efferent neuron, effector
B) Effector, efferent neuron, centre, afferent neuron, receptor
C) Afferent neuron, receptor, centre, effector, efferent neuron
D) Receptor, efferent neuron, centre, afferent neuron, effector
Ans: A
Topic: Basic Sciences
Diff: hard
Exp: A reflex arc begins at a receptor, travels along an afferent or sensory neuron to an integrating centre, then out via an efferent or motor neuron to an effector such as a muscle or gland. This pathway allows rapid, involuntary responses to stimuli.

Q: After originating in the SA node, the normal sequence of cardiac impulse conduction is
A) Purkinje fibres, AV node, bundle of His
B) AV node, Purkinje fibres, bundle of His
C) AV node, bundle of His, Purkinje fibres
D) Bundle of His, AV node, Purkinje fibres
Ans: C
Topic: Basic Sciences
Diff: hard
Exp: The impulse passes from the atrioventricular node to the bundle of His, then along its branches to the Purkinje fibres that excite the ventricular myocardium. The AV node introduces a brief delay allowing the atria to empty before the ventricles contract.

Q: Starling's law of the heart states that, within physiological limits, the force of ventricular contraction is
A) Directly proportional to the end-diastolic length of cardiac muscle fibres
B) Independent of the initial fibre length
C) Determined solely by the heart rate
D) Inversely proportional to venous return
Ans: A
Topic: Basic Sciences
Diff: hard
Exp: Starling's law states that the greater the end-diastolic stretch of cardiac muscle fibres, the greater the force of the subsequent contraction. This intrinsic mechanism allows the heart to match its output to increases in venous return.

Q: Mean arterial blood pressure is principally determined by the product of
A) Venous return and lung compliance
B) Heart rate and blood viscosity
C) Cardiac output and systemic vascular resistance
D) Stroke volume and haematocrit
Ans: C
Topic: Basic Sciences
Diff: medium
Exp: Mean arterial pressure is the product of cardiac output and systemic vascular resistance. A rise in either the amount of blood pumped or the resistance of the peripheral vessels will therefore raise the arterial blood pressure.

Q: The basic rhythm of breathing is generated by the respiratory centres located mainly in the
A) Cerebellum
B) Medulla oblongata and pons
C) Thalamus
D) Hypothalamus
Ans: B
Topic: Basic Sciences
Diff: medium
Exp: The medulla oblongata contains the fundamental rhythm-generating centres for respiration, with the pons providing fine modulation through its pneumotaxic and apneustic areas. These centres adjust ventilation in response to chemical and neural signals.

Q: The Hering-Breuer inflation reflex, which limits over-distension of the lungs, is mediated by afferent fibres travelling in the
A) Intercostal nerves
B) Vagus nerve
C) Phrenic nerve
D) Glossopharyngeal nerve
Ans: B
Topic: Basic Sciences
Diff: hard
Exp: Stretch receptors in the airways detect lung inflation and send impulses along the vagus nerve to the respiratory centre, inhibiting further inspiration. This Hering-Breuer reflex protects the lungs from excessive expansion.

Q: Gastrin, secreted by the G cells of the gastric antrum, chiefly stimulates
A) Contraction of the gall bladder

Ans: C
Topic: Basic Sciences
Diff: medium
Exp: Gastrin is released by antral G cells and stimulates the parietal cells to secrete hydrochloric acid, promoting gastric digestion. It also increases gastric motility and mucosal growth as part of preparing the stomach for a meal.

Q: Secretin, released from the duodenal mucosa in response to acidic chyme, stimulates the pancreas to secrete a fluid rich in
A) Digestive enzymes
B) Bicarbonate
C) Insulin
D) Bile pigments
Ans: B
Topic: Basic Sciences
Diff: hard
Exp: Secretin is released when acidic chyme enters the duodenum and drives the pancreas and biliary system to secrete a bicarbonate-rich alkaline fluid. This neutralises gastric acid and provides the optimal pH for intestinal enzymes.

Q: Which of the following hormones is secreted by the anterior lobe of the pituitary gland?

Q: Calcitonin, secreted by the parafollicular or C cells of the thyroid gland, acts primarily to
A) Raise plasma calcium concentration
B) Lower plasma calcium concentration
C) Increase intestinal absorption of calcium
D) Stimulate osteoclastic bone resorption
Ans: B
Topic: Basic Sciences
Diff: medium
Exp: Calcitonin lowers plasma calcium by inhibiting osteoclastic bone resorption and promoting calcium deposition in bone. Its action opposes that of parathyroid hormone, helping to fine-tune calcium balance when levels rise.

Q: Which plasma lipoprotein is commonly termed bad cholesterol owing to its role in depositing cholesterol in arterial walls?

B) Chylomicron
C) Intermediate-density lipoprotein
D) High-density lipoprotein
Ans: A
Topic: Basic Sciences

Exp: Low-density lipoprotein carries cholesterol to tissues and, in excess, deposits it in arterial walls, promoting atherosclerosis. High-density lipoprotein is protective because it transports cholesterol back to the liver for disposal.

Q: Deficiency of thiamine, vitamin B1, classically produces which disease?
A) Pellagra
B) Night blindness
C) Rickets
D) Beriberi
Ans: D
Topic: Basic Sciences
Diff: easy
Exp: Thiamine deficiency causes beriberi, which may present with peripheral neuropathy in the dry form or cardiac failure and oedema in the wet form. Thiamine acts as a coenzyme in carbohydrate metabolism, so nervous and cardiac tissues are especially affected.

Q: In eukaryotic cells, the reactions of the citric acid, or Krebs, cycle take place in the
A) Cytosol
B) Rough endoplasmic reticulum
C) Nucleus
D) Mitochondrial matrix
Ans: D
Topic: Basic Sciences
Diff: easy
Exp: The enzymes of the citric acid cycle are located in the mitochondrial matrix, where acetyl-CoA is oxidised to carbon dioxide. The reduced coenzymes produced then feed the electron transport chain on the inner mitochondrial membrane.

Q: The net gain of ATP from the anaerobic glycolysis of one molecule of glucose to pyruvate is
A) 4 ATP
B) 2 ATP
C) 36 ATP
D) 8 ATP
Ans: B
Topic: Basic Sciences
Diff: hard
Exp: Glycolysis produces four ATP by substrate-level phosphorylation but consumes two in the early steps, giving a net gain of two ATP per glucose. Additional ATP is generated only when pyruvate is further oxidised under aerobic conditions.

Topic: Basic Sciences
