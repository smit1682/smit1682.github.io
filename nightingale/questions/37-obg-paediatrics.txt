Q: The most common congenital heart disease in children is:
A) Tetralogy of Fallot
B) Patent ductus arteriosus
C) Ventricular septal defect
D) Atrial septal defect
Ans: C
Topic: Obstetrics & Child Health
Diff: easy
Exp: Ventricular septal defect is the single most common congenital heart defect, accounting for roughly a quarter to a third of all cases seen in children.

Q: Which of the following is a cyanotic congenital heart disease?
A) Patent ductus arteriosus
B) Tetralogy of Fallot
C) Ventricular septal defect
D) Atrial septal defect
Ans: B
Topic: Obstetrics & Child Health
Diff: easy
Exp: Tetralogy of Fallot causes right-to-left shunting and is a classic cyanotic lesion, whereas ASD, VSD and PDA are acyanotic left-to-right shunts.

Q: A continuous "machinery" murmur best heard below the left clavicle is characteristic of:
A) Atrial septal defect
B) Coarctation of the aorta
C) Patent ductus arteriosus
D) Ventricular septal defect
Ans: C
Topic: Obstetrics & Child Health
Diff: medium
Exp: A patent ductus arteriosus produces a continuous machinery murmur in the left infraclavicular area because abnormal flow persists through both systole and diastole.

Q: Upper-limb hypertension with weak, delayed femoral pulses (radio-femoral delay) suggests:
A) Ventricular septal defect
B) Coarctation of the aorta
C) Patent ductus arteriosus
D) Transposition of the great arteries
Ans: B
Topic: Obstetrics & Child Health
Diff: medium
Exp: Coarctation narrows the aorta, producing upper-limb hypertension with weak, delayed femoral pulses and a measurable pressure gradient between the arms and the legs.

Q: A wide, fixed splitting of the second heart sound is the classic auscultatory finding in:
A) Atrial septal defect
B) Ventricular septal defect
C) Pulmonary stenosis
D) Patent ductus arteriosus
Ans: A
Topic: Obstetrics & Child Health
Diff: medium
Exp: The wide, fixed splitting of the second heart sound reflects the constant left-to-right shunt across an atrial septal defect, which does not vary with respiration.

Q: Tetralogy of Fallot consists of a ventricular septal defect, overriding aorta, pulmonary stenosis and:
A) Patent ductus arteriosus
B) Right ventricular hypertrophy
C) Left ventricular hypertrophy
D) Atrial septal defect
Ans: B
Topic: Obstetrics & Child Health
Diff: easy
Exp: The tetralogy comprises a ventricular septal defect, overriding aorta, pulmonary stenosis and right ventricular hypertrophy that develops from the outflow obstruction.

Q: The commonest cyanotic congenital heart disease presenting in the first days of life is:
A) Total anomalous pulmonary venous connection
B) Tetralogy of Fallot
C) Tricuspid atresia
D) Transposition of the great arteries
Ans: D
Topic: Obstetrics & Child Health
Diff: hard
Exp: Transposition of the great arteries is the commonest cyanotic lesion presenting in the first days of life; survival depends on mixing through a PDA or a septal defect.

Q: During a hypercyanotic ("tet") spell, squatting relieves cyanosis mainly by:
A) Lowering pulmonary vascular resistance
B) Decreasing systemic venous return
C) Slowing the heart rate
D) Increasing systemic vascular resistance and reducing the right-to-left shunt
Ans: D
Topic: Obstetrics & Child Health
Diff: hard
Exp: Squatting kinks the femoral arteries and raises systemic vascular resistance, which reduces right-to-left shunting and forces more blood through the lungs, easing cyanosis.

Q: A barking cough with inspiratory stridor and a "steeple sign" on neck X-ray in a toddler indicates:

Ans: B
Topic: Obstetrics & Child Health

Exp: Croup is a viral (usually parainfluenza) infection producing a barking cough, inspiratory stridor and subglottic narrowing seen as the steeple sign on a neck radiograph.

Q: An aspirated foreign body in a child most commonly lodges in the:
A) Right main bronchus
B) Trachea
C) Left main bronchus
D) Larynx
Ans: A
Topic: Obstetrics & Child Health
Diff: medium
Exp: An aspirated foreign body most often enters the right main bronchus because it is wider, shorter and more vertical than the left, making it the path of least resistance.

Topic: Obstetrics & Child Health

Q: Diarrhoea with visible blood in the stools (dysentery) in a child is most commonly caused by:

Ans: D
Topic: Obstetrics & Child Health

Q: Coeliac disease in children is caused by an immune reaction to:
A) Fructose
B) Lactose
C) Gluten (gliadin)
D) Cow's milk protein
Ans: C
Topic: Obstetrics & Child Health
Diff: hard
Exp: Coeliac disease is an immune-mediated enteropathy triggered by the gliadin fraction of gluten, causing villous atrophy; anti-tissue transglutaminase antibodies aid diagnosis.

Q: "Red-currant-jelly" stools with a palpable sausage-shaped abdominal mass in an infant suggest:

D) Hirschsprung disease
Ans: C
Topic: Obstetrics & Child Health
Diff: medium
Exp: Intussusception, the telescoping of one bowel segment into another, classically produces red-currant-jelly stools, colicky pain and a palpable sausage-shaped mass in infants.

Q: Hirschsprung disease is characterized by:
A) Absence of ganglion cells in the distal bowel
B) Hypertrophy of the pyloric muscle
C) Telescoping of one bowel segment into another
D) Villous atrophy of the small intestine
Ans: A
Topic: Obstetrics & Child Health
Diff: medium
Exp: Hirschsprung disease results from failed migration of neural crest cells, leaving the distal bowel aganglionic; it presents with delayed passage of meconium and constipation.

Q: Long-standing large left-to-right shunts (e.g., VSD) may lead to Eisenmenger syndrome, which is:

D) Development of infective endocarditis
Ans: C
Topic: Obstetrics & Child Health
Diff: hard
Exp: Persistent large left-to-right shunts raise pulmonary vascular resistance until the shunt reverses to right-to-left (Eisenmenger syndrome), producing cyanosis and precluding surgical repair.
