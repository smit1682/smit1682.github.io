# Batch 8 — Obstetrics and Child Health, second set

Q: A woman who has had three pregnancies and two live births is recorded as
A) Gravida 5, para 3
B) Gravida 3, para 3
C) Gravida 2, para 3
D) Gravida 3, para 2
Ans: D
Topic: Obstetrics
Diff: medium
Exp: Gravida counts all pregnancies including the current one, and para counts births after the age of viability. So three pregnancies with two births is gravida 3, para 2.

Q: Painless bright red vaginal bleeding in the third trimester most suggests
A) Abruptio placentae
B) Cervical polyp
C) Ruptured uterus
D) Placenta praevia
Ans: D
Topic: Obstetrics
Diff: hard
Exp: Praevia bleeds painlessly because the low-lying placenta separates as the lower segment forms, and the uterus stays soft. Abruption gives painful bleeding with a tense tender uterus, and vaginal examination is avoided in suspected praevia until placental site is known.

Q: The commonest site of an ectopic pregnancy is the
A) Fallopian tube
B) Abdominal cavity
C) Cervix
D) Ovary
Ans: A
Topic: Obstetrics
Diff: medium
Exp: About 95 per cent of ectopic pregnancies implant in the fallopian tube, most often the ampulla. The classic presentation is amenorrhoea, lower abdominal pain and spotting, and rupture causes sudden severe pain with shock.

Q: Anti-D immunoglobulin is given to
A) All mothers regardless of blood group
B) The newborn immediately after birth
C) Rh negative mothers who are not already sensitised, after delivery of an Rh positive baby or a sensitising event
D) Rh positive mothers with an Rh negative baby
Ans: C
Topic: Obstetrics
Diff: hard
Exp: Anti-D destroys fetal red cells that have entered the maternal circulation before the mother can form her own antibodies, and is given within 72 hours of delivery or a sensitising event such as miscarriage or amniocentesis. It cannot help a mother who is already sensitised.

Q: The partograph is used mainly to
A) Assess the newborn at birth
B) Calculate the expected date of delivery
C) Record the antenatal history
D) Monitor the progress of labour and detect deviation early
Ans: D
Topic: Obstetrics
Diff: medium
Exp: The partograph plots cervical dilatation against time together with fetal and maternal observations. Crossing the alert line signals slow progress and crossing the action line calls for definitive intervention.

Q: Lochia rubra, serosa and alba normally appear in the order
A) Whitish, then red, then brown
B) Brown, then white, then red
C) Red, then pink-brown, then whitish
D) They appear simultaneously
Ans: C
Topic: Obstetrics
Diff: medium
Exp: Lochia rubra is red for about the first three to four days, becoming serosa, pinkish brown, for up to about ten days, and finally alba, whitish, for a further one to two weeks. A return to red bleeding or an offensive smell suggests retained products or infection.

Q: The uterus normally returns to its pre-pregnant size by about
A) Six weeks after delivery
B) One week after delivery
C) Three months after delivery
D) Six months after delivery
Ans: A
Topic: Obstetrics
Diff: medium
Exp: Involution takes roughly six weeks, with the fundus descending about one centimetre a day and becoming impalpable abdominally by around ten days. A uterus that stays high and boggy suggests subinvolution.

Q: Puerperal sepsis is defined as infection of the genital tract occurring
A) Only after caesarean section
B) During pregnancy only
C) Only in the first 24 hours
D) Any time from rupture of membranes or labour up to 42 days postpartum
Ans: D
Topic: Obstetrics
Diff: hard
Exp: The definition covers infection from rupture of membranes or the onset of labour until 42 days after delivery, with fever, offensive lochia, uterine tenderness and delayed involution. It remains a leading direct cause of maternal death.

Q: A woman in labour has a cord prolapse. The immediate nursing action is
A) Apply a tight vaginal pack
B) Ask the woman to sit upright and push
C) Relieve pressure on the cord by positioning, keep it warm and moist, and call for immediate delivery
D) Push the cord back into the uterus
Ans: C
Topic: Obstetrics
Diff: hard
Exp: The cord must not be handled or replaced, as this causes spasm. The presenting part is lifted off the cord, the woman is placed knee-chest or in exaggerated Sims position with the hips raised, and preparations are made for immediate caesarean section.

Q: The copper-bearing intrauterine device works chiefly by
A) Blocking the fallopian tubes mechanically
B) Preventing ovulation entirely
C) Creating a local inflammatory and spermicidal environment that prevents fertilisation
D) Suppressing pituitary hormones
Ans: C
Topic: Obstetrics
Diff: hard
Exp: Copper ions are toxic to sperm and provoke a sterile inflammatory reaction in the endometrium, preventing fertilisation. It does not reliably stop ovulation, and it offers no protection against sexually transmitted infection.

Q: Combined oral contraceptive pills are generally contraindicated in a woman who
A) Has mild dysmenorrhoea
B) Is breastfeeding a six-week-old baby or has a history of thromboembolism
C) Is 22 years old and nulliparous
D) Has irregular but ovulatory cycles
Ans: B
Topic: Obstetrics
Diff: hard
Exp: Oestrogen raises the risk of venous thromboembolism and can reduce milk supply in early lactation, so progestogen-only methods are preferred in these situations. Smoking over the age of 35 and migraine with aura are other important contraindications.

Q: The minimum number of antenatal visits recommended for a normal pregnancy under Indian national guidance is
A) Four
B) Two
C) Ten
D) One
Ans: A
Topic: Obstetrics
Diff: medium
Exp: At least four visits are recommended, the first in the first trimester, with WHO now advising eight contacts for better outcomes. Each visit checks weight, blood pressure, urine, haemoglobin, fundal height and fetal heart, along with tetanus prophylaxis and iron and folic acid.

Q: Kangaroo mother care involves
A) Continuous skin-to-skin contact on the mother's chest with exclusive breastfeeding
B) Feeding by nasogastric tube only
C) Nursing the baby in an incubator at all times
D) Isolating the baby from the mother
Ans: A
Topic: Child Health
Diff: medium
Exp: Skin-to-skin contact stabilises the baby's temperature, heart rate and breathing, promotes breastfeeding and bonding, and reduces infection and mortality in low birth weight babies. It is effective, and free.

Q: A baby weighing less than 2500 grams at birth is classified as
A) Normal weight
B) Extremely low birth weight
C) Low birth weight
D) Very low birth weight
Ans: C
Topic: Child Health
Diff: easy
Exp: Low birth weight is under 2500 grams, very low under 1500 and extremely low under 1000. Low birth weight results either from prematurity or from intrauterine growth restriction, and the distinction matters for management.

Q: A healthy infant can usually sit without support by about
A) 2 months
B) 4 months
C) 6 to 8 months
D) 15 months
Ans: C
Topic: Child Health
Diff: medium
Exp: Sitting without support comes at around six to eight months, standing with support at about nine, and independent walking between twelve and fifteen months. Not walking by eighteen months warrants assessment.

Q: Complementary feeding should be started at
A) 2 months
B) 4 months
C) 6 months
D) 12 months
Ans: C
Topic: Child Health
Diff: easy
Exp: Breast milk alone no longer meets energy and iron needs after six months, so semi-solid complementary foods begin then while breastfeeding continues. Starting earlier displaces breast milk and raises infection risk; starting later risks growth faltering.

Q: The current WHO oral rehydration solution is described as
A) Reduced osmolarity, containing sodium, potassium, chloride, citrate and glucose
B) High osmolarity with high sodium
C) Sodium bicarbonate solution alone
D) Plain glucose water
Ans: A
Topic: Child Health
Diff: hard
Exp: The reduced osmolarity formula lowers stool output, vomiting and the need for intravenous fluids compared with the older solution. Glucose is essential because sodium absorption in the gut is coupled to glucose transport.

Q: Under the national programme, vitamin A prophylaxis for children is given
A) Daily from birth
B) Once in a lifetime
C) Every six months from nine months up to five years
D) Only when night blindness appears
Ans: C
Topic: Child Health
Diff: hard
Exp: The first dose accompanies measles vaccination at nine months, then 200,000 IU every six months until five years, for a total of nine doses. Vitamin A deficiency remains a leading preventable cause of childhood blindness and worsens measles and diarrhoea.

Q: Tetralogy of Fallot consists of pulmonary stenosis, overriding aorta, right ventricular hypertrophy and
A) Atrial septal defect
B) Coarctation of the aorta
C) Ventricular septal defect
D) Patent ductus arteriosus
Ans: C
Topic: Child Health
Diff: hard
Exp: The fourth component is a ventricular septal defect. It is the commonest cyanotic congenital heart disease beyond infancy, and children characteristically squat during hypercyanotic spells because it raises systemic resistance and improves pulmonary flow.

Q: The classic presentation of nephrotic syndrome in a child is
A) Heavy proteinuria, hypoalbuminaemia, oedema and hyperlipidaemia
B) Fever with dysuria only
C) Polyuria and weight loss
D) Haematuria, hypertension and oliguria with minimal protein
Ans: A
Topic: Child Health
Diff: hard
Exp: Nephrotic syndrome is dominated by protein loss, giving low albumin, generalised oedema often first noticed as puffy eyelids, and raised lipids. The nephritic picture, by contrast, is dominated by haematuria, hypertension and reduced output.

Q: A child with acute epiglottitis should not
A) Be kept calm and upright
B) Receive oxygen
C) Be taken to theatre for airway assessment
D) Have the throat examined with a spatula
Ans: D
Topic: Child Health
Diff: hard
Exp: Examining the throat can provoke complete airway obstruction. The child is kept calm, upright and undisturbed, ideally with a parent, while senior anaesthetic and ENT help is summoned to secure the airway.

Q: Rheumatic fever most commonly follows infection with
A) Staphylococcus aureus
B) Candida albicans
C) Group A beta-haemolytic Streptococcus
D) Escherichia coli
Ans: C
Topic: Child Health
Diff: medium
Exp: Rheumatic fever follows a throat infection with group A Streptococcus by two to three weeks, through an immune cross-reaction that can permanently damage heart valves. Treating streptococcal sore throat properly is the primary prevention.

Q: The Apgar score is routinely assessed at
A) 1 and 5 minutes after birth
B) At 24 hours
C) Only at 10 minutes
D) Every hour for six hours
Ans: A
Topic: Child Health
Diff: easy
Exp: The score is taken at one and five minutes, and repeated every five minutes up to twenty if it remains below seven. It describes the transition to extrauterine life and should never delay resuscitation, which begins on clinical assessment.

Q: A newborn's temperature is best maintained immediately after birth by
A) Drying thoroughly, removing wet linen and placing skin to skin under a warm cover
B) Leaving the baby uncovered under a fan
C) Placing the baby directly on a cold surface
D) Bathing the baby at once
Ans: A
Topic: Child Health
Diff: medium
Exp: Newborns lose heat rapidly by evaporation, conduction, convection and radiation. Drying and removing wet cloth, skin-to-skin contact and covering the head prevent hypothermia, and the first bath is delayed for at least 24 hours.
