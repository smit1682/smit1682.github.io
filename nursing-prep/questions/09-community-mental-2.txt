# Batch 9 — Community Health and Mental Health, second set

Q: In the plains, one sub-centre in India is established for a population of about
A) 1000
B) 5000
C) 30000
D) 100000
Ans: B
Topic: Community Health
Diff: medium
Exp: A sub-centre serves about 5000 people in the plains and 3000 in hilly, tribal or difficult areas. A primary health centre covers about 30,000 and 20,000 respectively, and a community health centre about 120,000.

Q: The crude birth rate is expressed as the number of live births per
A) 100000 women
B) 1000 live births
C) 1000 mid-year population
D) 100 population
Ans: C
Topic: Community Health
Diff: medium
Exp: Crude birth rate uses the mid-year population as its denominator. It is called crude because it takes no account of age or sex structure, unlike the general fertility rate which uses women of reproductive age.

Q: Case fatality rate measures
A) Deaths in the whole population
B) The number of new cases in a year
C) The number of existing cases at a point in time
D) Deaths from a disease among those who have that disease
Ans: D
Topic: Community Health
Diff: hard
Exp: Case fatality rate is deaths from a disease divided by the number of diagnosed cases, expressing the virulence of that disease. Incidence counts new cases over a period and prevalence counts all existing cases at a point.

Q: The ability of a screening test to correctly identify those who truly have the disease is its
A) Specificity
B) Predictive value
C) Sensitivity
D) Reliability
Ans: C
Topic: Community Health
Diff: hard
Exp: Sensitivity is the proportion of true cases the test detects, so a highly sensitive test produces few false negatives and is preferred for screening. Specificity is the ability to correctly exclude those without the disease.

Q: The incubation period of a disease is the interval between
A) Two successive cases in a chain of transmission
B) Exposure to the agent and the appearance of the first symptoms
C) Diagnosis and treatment
D) Onset of symptoms and recovery
Ans: B
Topic: Community Health
Diff: medium
Exp: Incubation runs from entry of the organism to the first clinical signs. The generation time, by contrast, is the interval between successive cases and matters more when tracking an outbreak.

Q: Under the ICDS scheme, services are delivered mainly through the
A) Private clinic
B) Medical college
C) District hospital
D) Anganwadi centre
Ans: D
Topic: Community Health
Diff: easy
Exp: The Integrated Child Development Services scheme works through the anganwadi centre and worker, providing supplementary nutrition, immunisation, health check-ups, referral, pre-school education and nutrition and health education to children under six and to pregnant and nursing mothers.

Q: Janani Suraksha Yojana was introduced primarily to
A) Fund medical education
B) Provide free medicines to the elderly
C) Supply drinking water
D) Promote institutional delivery through conditional cash assistance
Ans: D
Topic: Community Health
Diff: medium
Exp: JSY gives cash assistance to encourage delivery in a health facility, aiming to reduce maternal and neonatal mortality. It works alongside the ASHA, who accompanies the mother and receives a linked incentive.

Q: Universal salt iodisation is the main strategy against
A) Iron deficiency anaemia
B) Protein energy malnutrition
C) Iodine deficiency disorders including goitre and cretinism
D) Vitamin A deficiency
Ans: C
Topic: Community Health
Diff: medium
Exp: Iodised salt is the cornerstone of prevention, with iodine content specified at production and at the household level. Deficiency in pregnancy causes irreversible mental impairment in the child, which is why prevention rather than treatment is the goal.

Q: Sterilisation as a family planning method is best described as
A) Requiring daily action by the user
B) Effective only in women
C) Permanent, with tubectomy for women and vasectomy for men
D) Temporary and easily reversible
Ans: C
Topic: Community Health
Diff: easy
Exp: Tubectomy and vasectomy are permanent methods and should be chosen only when the family is complete. Vasectomy is simpler, safer and cheaper, but a man remains fertile until the stored sperm are cleared, so another method is needed for about three months.

Q: A disease that must be reported to public health authorities by law is termed
A) Notifiable
B) Idiopathic
C) Sporadic
D) Endemic
Ans: A
Topic: Community Health
Diff: easy
Exp: Notifiable diseases such as cholera, tuberculosis and measles must be reported so that outbreaks can be detected and controlled. Prompt notification by nursing staff is often the first step in an outbreak response.

Q: The most effective household method of making water bacteriologically safe where fuel is available is
A) Adding alum only
B) Storing in a covered pot for a day
C) Filtering through a cloth
D) Boiling for a rolling boil of several minutes
Ans: D
Topic: Community Health
Diff: medium
Exp: Boiling kills bacteria, viruses and cysts reliably. Alum only helps sedimentation of suspended matter, and cloth filtration removes larger particles and cyclops but not bacteria.

Q: Primary health care as defined at Alma-Ata rests on principles that include
A) Payment at the point of care
B) Care limited to hospitals
C) Treatment by specialists only
D) Equitable distribution, community participation, intersectoral coordination and appropriate technology
Ans: D
Topic: Community Health
Diff: hard
Exp: The four pillars are equitable distribution, community participation, intersectoral coordination and appropriate technology, with prevention emphasised over cure. They remain the framework for national health policy.

Q: Panic level anxiety is characterised by
A) Mild restlessness only
B) Disorganised behaviour, distorted perception and inability to function
C) Narrowed perception with some ability to focus if directed
D) Heightened alertness and improved learning
Ans: B
Topic: Mental Health
Diff: medium
Exp: Anxiety runs from mild, which sharpens attention, through moderate and severe with progressively narrowing perception, to panic, where the person cannot process the environment or act purposefully and needs a calm presence and a safe, quiet setting.

Q: Obsessive-compulsive disorder is characterised by
A) Loss of memory for personal identity
B) Hearing voices that others cannot hear
C) Recurrent intrusive thoughts recognised as one's own, with repetitive acts performed to reduce anxiety
D) Fixed false beliefs held with conviction
Ans: C
Topic: Mental Health
Diff: medium
Exp: The person knows the thoughts are their own and senseless but cannot dismiss them, and rituals relieve anxiety only briefly. This insight distinguishes obsessions from delusions.

Q: A patient in an acute manic episode is best nursed by
A) Encouraging participation in competitive group games
B) Leaving the patient unobserved to burn off energy
C) Confronting grandiose statements directly and arguing
D) Providing a calm low-stimulus environment, finger foods and short frequent contacts
Ans: D
Topic: Mental Health
Diff: hard
Exp: Overstimulation worsens mania, and the patient is often too restless to sit for meals, so portable high-calorie finger foods maintain nutrition. Arguing about grandiosity provokes hostility; setting firm consistent limits works better.

Q: Delirium tremens following alcohol withdrawal typically appears
A) Within one hour of the last drink
B) Two weeks later
C) Between 48 and 96 hours after the last drink
D) Only after six months of abstinence
Ans: C
Topic: Mental Health
Diff: hard
Exp: Tremor and agitation begin within about 6 to 24 hours, but delirium tremens with confusion, vivid hallucinations, fever and autonomic instability peaks at two to four days and can be fatal. Benzodiazepines and thiamine are the mainstays of management.

Q: Wernicke encephalopathy in chronic alcohol use is caused by deficiency of
A) Calcium
B) Vitamin C
C) Vitamin D
D) Thiamine
Ans: D
Topic: Mental Health
Diff: hard
Exp: Thiamine deficiency produces confusion, ataxia and eye signs, and thiamine must be given before any glucose load, since giving glucose first can precipitate the syndrome. Untreated it may progress to irreversible Korsakoff psychosis.

Q: Physical restraint of a psychiatric patient is acceptable only when
A) The ward is short staffed
B) A relative requests it
C) All less restrictive measures have failed and there is immediate risk of harm, with documentation and frequent review
D) The patient is uncooperative at mealtimes
Ans: C
Topic: Mental Health
Diff: medium
Exp: Restraint is a last resort, used for the shortest possible time to prevent harm, with the least restrictive method, continuous observation, attention to circulation, hydration and elimination, and clear documentation. It is never a substitute for staffing or a punishment.

Q: Anorexia nervosa is characterised by
A) Loss of appetite due to physical illness
B) Refusal to maintain body weight with intense fear of gaining weight and disturbed body image
C) Craving for non-food substances
D) Episodes of overeating followed by guilt but normal weight
Ans: B
Topic: Mental Health
Diff: medium
Exp: The core features are self-imposed starvation, intense fear of weight gain and a distorted perception of body size, with amenorrhoea and dangerous cardiac and electrolyte complications. Craving non-food substances is pica.

Q: The Mental Healthcare Act 2017 in India is notable for
A) Removing all rights from persons with mental illness
B) Making institutional admission compulsory
C) Recognising the right to access mental healthcare and effectively decriminalising attempted suicide
D) Banning community treatment
Ans: C
Topic: Mental Health
Diff: hard
Exp: The Act asserts a right to access affordable mental healthcare, allows advance directives and nominated representatives, and presumes that a person attempting suicide is under severe stress, so they are not punished. It represents a decisive shift towards rights-based care.
